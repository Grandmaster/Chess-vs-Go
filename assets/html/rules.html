Chess-VS-Go

Game Home Lobby

The game available on this site is a mix between the classic games of Chess and Go(or Weiqi/Baduk). I came up with it when noticing that stones are played on points and pieces on squares, so the two games could coexist on the same board. This page details the rules of the game that are not part of the basic ruleset of Chess and Go. Of course, in order to play this game, you must at the very least know how to play both Chess and Go at the beginner level. To learn online, for chess I would recommend lichess, an online chess site with many players and a suite of tools to help newcomers learn the game. For Go, a similar site is online-go, with both of these sites being completely free to join and play. The rest of this page assumes that you know the basic rules of both of these games, because all rules of each game also apply in this one.


Basic game structure

This section details the moves a player can make during each turn over the course of the game. At the beginning:

  • Turn 1: With no stones and no pieces on the board, your first move must be to place the king on the board. No other move will be accepted at this stage. There are rules to placing pieces on the board, but they are ignored this turn so that the game may begin. To place your king (or any other piece) on the board, click on the corresponding piece on your bench, then click on the square you want to place your piece on.
  • Early Turns: At this point in the game, the only piece you will have out is your king, so the only moves you can make are to either move your king to a different square, or place a stone on the board. To place a stone, click on the destination point. To move your king (or any other piece), click on the piece, then click on one of the highlighted squares that indicate its range.
  • With at least one Zone on the Board: When you have one or more zones on the board (more on them below) you gain the ability to place a piece from your bench on the board. You can only place pieces from your bench onto zones you control. To place a piece, click on the piece, then click on any square that is a zone you control.
  • With at least one Enemy Piece/Stone on the Board: You can capture enemy pieces and capture/destroy enemy stones with your pieces/stones. There are many ways to do this, which will be explained below.

With a few exceptions, you can only do one of these things per turn. The game ends when your king is killed (there is no check).


Pieces

This section details the extra abilities chess pieces have in this game, in addition to their normal abilities. They usually relate to interactions with stones on the board.

  • King: In addition to his normal abilities, whenever the king moves from one square to another, it can move a friendly stone on one of the corners of the landing square to an empty corner on the same square. The king can basically move stones already placed on the board, and it is the only piece that can do so.
  • Official: In addition to all their other abilities, when an official moves from one square to another, it has the ability to convert an enemy stone on a corner of its landing square to a friendly stone.
  • Pawn: In addition to all its normal abilities, the pawn can capture stones diagonally. That means that the pawn can capture a stone that shares a corner with both its starting square and its landing square if the pawn were to capture diagonally. Also, pawns can move one step in any cardinal direction. Refer to images on the side.

Stones

This section details the extra abilities stones have in this game, in addition to their inherited abilities. They relate to the way stones interact with pieces in the game.

  • Zones: Stones can form zones on squares on the board. A square becomes a zone of a player if the square either has four stones of the player's color on all four corners, or a corner is a point that exists as territory (the Go kind) belonging to the player. A piece is immediately captured if it exists in a zone of the opposite color. Refer to images.
  • Walls: When two stones of the same color are adjacent to each other, they form a wall that can restrict the ranges of pieces. Refer to images.
  • Empty Triangle: A stone that forms the corner of an empty triangle cannot be captured by a pawn, or jumped over by a king.
